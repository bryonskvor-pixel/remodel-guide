The Home
Addition Guide

A home addition is the most complex residential project a homeowner can undertake. More moving parts. More trades. More inspections. More decisions. More ways things can go wrong — and more ways things can go right when the right contractor is selected and the right expectations are set from the beginning. This guide is a protection guide — what to understand before anyone is hired and before a shovel goes in the ground.

What a home addition actually demands

"A home addition is not a remodel. It is new construction attached to an existing structure — and it demands the knowledge, experience, and project management capability of a contractor who has done it many times before."

Every other project type on remodel.guide involves changing something that exists. A home addition creates something from nothing — new foundation, new framing, new roof, new systems — while connecting it to an existing structure that has its own age, quirks, and conditions.

The connection point between old and new is where additions most commonly fail — structurally, aesthetically, and from a water management perspective. Getting that connection right requires experience that cannot be faked and cannot be learned on a single project.

Home additions done well are transformative. They add space, value, and livability in ways that no other project can replicate. The homeowner who goes in with accurate expectations and the right contractor has an excellent experience. The homeowner who underestimates the complexity or hires the wrong contractor has one of the worst experiences in residential construction.

Contractor Selection

The most important decision
in any addition project.

On most remodeling projects, contractor selection matters a great deal. On a home addition, it is the primary variable that determines whether the project succeeds or fails. The difference between a contractor with 3 additions completed and one with 30 is not incremental. It is categorical.

✓

Ask specifically how many additions they have completed — and visit one

Not remodels. Additions. A contractor with a strong kitchen remodel portfolio is not necessarily qualified to manage an addition. Ask the number. Ask to visit completed projects. Look at the connection point — where the new structure meets the existing house. Look at the roofline transition. That visual tells you more than any reference call.

✓

Confirm they pull their own permits and manage their own subcontractors

An addition requires a general contractor who owns the permit and is responsible for every inspection. A contractor who suggests the homeowner pull the permit is asking the homeowner to take on legal responsibility they are not equipped to manage. The GC owns the permit. Full stop.

⚠️

The lowest bid on an addition is the most dangerous bid of any project type

A low bid on an addition might mean an under-experienced contractor, missing scope items, or a contractor who has never managed the complexity of permitting, inspection sequencing, and structural connection work. Get three bids. Understand specifically what is different between them.

Foundation Type

The decision that starts
everything else.

Before framing can be designed, before permits can be drawn — the foundation type must be determined. This decision affects cost, timeline, the usability of the new space, and whether the addition is feasible where it's planned.

Option 1

Slab Foundation

Concrete poured directly on grade. Fastest and least expensive. No crawl space or basement underneath. Appropriate for single-story additions where grade permits. Requires careful attention to drainage to prevent water intrusion at the slab edge.

Option 2

Crawl Space

Foundation walls with low clearance below the addition floor. Provides access to plumbing and mechanicals. Must be properly ventilated or encapsulated. Adds height to the addition floor relative to grade — a transition detail that must be resolved at the connection to the existing house.

Option 3

Full Basement

Most expensive foundation option — but adds the most usable square footage. Requires significant excavation. Longest timeline of the three options. Requires careful waterproofing and drainage planning — the same moisture conversation that applies to any basement applies here.

⚠️

Footings must go below frost line in Ohio — 36 to 42 inches

Any foundation for an addition requires footings that extend below frost line. A contractor who pours shallow footings to save cost is creating a foundation that will heave seasonally. This is a code requirement and an inspection item. Confirm footing depth is in the drawings before permits are submitted.

Permits and Timeline

The reality nobody
warns you about.

Home additions always require permits. In every municipality. Without exception. The permit process — architectural drawings, structural engineering, plan review, and inspections at multiple stages — takes significantly longer than most homeowners expect.

Months 1–2

Design and drawings

Architectural drawings are required for permit submission on virtually all addition projects. A licensed architect or designer produces construction documents. This process takes 4 to 8 weeks depending on complexity. Nothing moves to permit without completed drawings.

Months 2–4

Permit submission and plan review

In most Northeast Ohio municipalities, plan review for a home addition takes 4 to 8 weeks. This timeline cannot be compressed — it is determined by the building department's workload, not the contractor's schedule. A homeowner who expects to break ground six weeks after deciding to build an addition has not accounted for this phase.

Month 4+

Construction — with inspections at every stage

Inspections at footing, foundation, framing, rough-in, insulation, and drywall each gate the next phase. A failed inspection adds days or weeks. A missed inspection discovered later requires opening work that has been covered.

Total

Six months minimum — plan for nine to twelve

A straightforward single-story addition in Northeast Ohio, from design decision to certificate of occupancy, runs six months at minimum when everything goes well. A more complex addition runs nine to twelve months or more.

Matching the Existing House

The detail that separates
an addition from a bump-out.

A home addition that looks like an addition is a design failure. The goal is a house that looks like it was always that size. Achieving that requires attention to details that add cost but produce results that look intentional.

Roofline — the most visible connection

How the new roof connects to the existing roof is the most visible indicator of whether an addition was well-designed. A roofline that continues the pitch and profile of the existing house looks intentional. A roofline that changes pitch or creates a visual break looks like two structures joined together. This must be addressed in the architectural drawings.

Exterior finish — matching is harder than it looks

Vinyl siding products change. Wood siding weathers. Brick color varies by production run. A new addition clad in nominally matching siding that is slightly different in profile, texture, or color looks like a patch. Options include replacing all siding on the affected elevation, choosing a complementary but intentionally different material, or accepting visible difference as a design feature. Confirm with physical samples before committing.

Windows, trim, and interior transition

Windows on the addition should match the style and proportion of existing windows. Interior floor, ceiling height, and trim transitions at the connection point must be resolved in the design — not improvised during construction.

Grading and Drainage

The site work nobody
thinks about until it rains.

Excavation for an addition displaces significant soil. Where that soil goes, how grade is established, and how water drains away from both the new foundation and the connection point — these decisions determine whether the addition stays dry or becomes a moisture problem.

Positive drainage away from the foundation — grade that slopes away from the house at a minimum of one inch per foot for the first six feet — is required by code. The connection point between the existing and new foundation is particularly vulnerable and requires explicit waterproofing and drainage planning.

⚠️

Final grade and drainage must be explicitly in the contract scope

A contract that says "excavation and foundation" without addressing final grade is a contract with a gap that will be filled at the homeowner's expense.

Budget Reality

What additions actually cost
in Northeast Ohio.

A general range for a quality addition in Northeast Ohio: $150 to $300+ per square foot all-in, including design, permits, foundation, framing, mechanicals, insulation, drywall, finishes, and exterior work. A 400-square-foot addition at the middle of that range is a $200,000 project.

What drives the number higher: a bathroom adds plumbing and tile cost. A kitchen adds everything. A second story adds structural complexity and a stair. A basement foundation adds excavation and concrete. Matching difficult existing materials adds labor and material cost.

⚠️

Contingency for additions — 15 to 20 percent minimum

Additions surface surprises more than any other project type — excavation, opening existing walls, connecting to a structure whose full condition is not known until work begins. Unexpected soil conditions, structural issues, outdated wiring or plumbing that must be brought to code. A 15 to 20 percent contingency is responsible planning, not pessimism.

Questions to Ask Every Contractor

The questions that reveal
who has actually done this before.

"How many home additions have you completed — and can I visit one or two?"

The primary qualification question. A specific number and willingness to show completed projects distinguishes experienced addition contractors from those who primarily do remodels.

"Who pulls the permit and who is the licensed contractor of record?"

The general contractor owns the permit. A contractor who asks the homeowner to pull the permit is transferring legal and liability risk to someone not equipped to manage it.

"How will the roofline connect to the existing structure?"

A contractor with a specific, thought-through answer has designed additions before. A vague answer indicates someone who hasn't considered the visual outcome.

"How will you match the existing exterior finish?"

The honest answer acknowledges the challenge and proposes a specific approach. "We'll get it close" is not an answer.

"What is your plan for grading and drainage around the new foundation?"

Reveals whether the contractor thinks about long-term performance or just the construction phase.

"What is your labor warranty on addition work?"

Addition work involves structural connections, roofing, and foundation work. A minimum 2-year labor warranty on all work is reasonable. Get it in writing.
